Defining disability

Before we delve further into our discussion of accessibility, it is important that we clarify the lens through which we view disability. This module covers two primary models that describe the overarching ways in which society views disability: the medical model and the social model.

The Medical Model

The medical model defines disability as a deficit of physical, sensory, cognitive, or emotional function, measured against standards for what the medical community regards as “normal”. This model offers medical professionals and governments a universal definition of what is considered a disability, which is used for diagnosing individuals with disability and making policies or programs for disabled people.

However, when applied beyond medicine and policymaking, the medical model causes more harm than good for the disabled community. This model of disability causes society to view disabled people as “less than” their abled counterparts, leading to their stigmatization. Disabled individuals are asked to provide proof of their disability to receive accommodations, which results in unequal access to spaces, both physical and digital. The medical model’s deficit-based view of disability has given rise to the invention of tools meant to “assist” disabled people in adapting to a world built without their needs in mind— such as stair climbing wheelchairs and sign language interpreting gloves— that are often too costly or inefficient to be usable by their intended audiences.

The Social Model

Alternatively, the social model defines disability as the state of being impaired by one’s environment and society— it is the individual’s surroundings that disable them, not their innate characteristics or capabilities. It is the poor planning of buildings that bars users of wheelchairs from entering, the inaccessible design of websites that makes them unusable to users of screen readers, and the public's perception of disabled people that excludes them from society.

The social model is critical in that it places the onus on architects and web designers to account for the needs of disabled users, rather than placing the expectation on disabled people to find their way around barriers in their environment. In this project, we will be approaching digital accessibility and web design through this lens.
