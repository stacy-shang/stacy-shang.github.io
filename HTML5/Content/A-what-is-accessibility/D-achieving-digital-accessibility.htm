Achieving digital accessibility

Designing accessible websites

When it comes to web design, it is important to consider some basic web and writing accessibility best practices:

  • Alternative text: writing short, descriptive alt text for images to make them accessible to users of screen readers

  • Semantic HTML: using the proper tags, especially for elements such as heading levels, to give context these features to screen readers

  • Color contrast: choosing colors that have sufficient contrast to ensure that text is distinguishable from the background for readers with low vision

  • Typography: choosing more legible fonts and font sizes for users with low vision or cognitive disabilities such as dyslexia

  • Responsiveness: designing your website to be usable on different browsers or devices

  • Skimmability: using sections with descriptive headings and subheadings to make your content more easy to scan

  • Plain language: writing using broad language, avoiding use of technical jargon, can help your audience better understand your content

  • Accessibility checkers: running your website through an accessibility checker can help identify potential accessibility issues

Conducting user research

Bad, inaccessible design is often the result of designers not considering or understanding the needs of users. Although accessibility checkers can check your website’s compliance with accessibility guidelines, they cannot account for the diverse needs of individual users. This is where user research comes in.

Conducting user interviews can be a helpful way to gauge the needs of the user base and plan the design and contents of a website. Usability testing can provide insight on specific pain points users encounter in navigating a website. Inclusion of a diverse range of users, including, and especially, those with disabilities, during these stages is vital to understanding the needs of your user base.

Creating accessible video content

The remaining sections of this project will focus on important concepts, best practices, and how-to’s related to creating accessible video content, including:

  • Video transcripts: converting audio content into written content allows people with hearing issues or who cannot currently listen to the audio to engage with the content

  • Closed captions: providing a written version of spoken dialogue, as well as non-verbal features like sound effects and music cues make videos accessible to deaf and hard of hearing audiences

  • Audio description: describing facial expressions, diagrams, body language, and text on the screen allows for people with visual impairments to understand important visual information in a video

  • Visual formatting: adding static visual components, such as title cards, can build structure and recap information already covered in the video

  • Transition effects: including transitions in your video can add visual flow, maintain clarity, and enhance understanding for your audience

  • Chunking, chapters, and on-screen markup: implementing these features can allow your audience to better digest information and understand visuals in the video
