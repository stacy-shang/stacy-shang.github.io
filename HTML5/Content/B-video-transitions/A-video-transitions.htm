Video transitions and accessibility

Transitions can be an important part of your video. They are used to organize visual flow, maintain clarity, and enhance understanding. Transitions add another layer of visual aesthetics and organization that can be extremely useful to your audience. They make your video feel smoother and in product design, can simulate a timeline. More benefits of transitions include:

  • Attention capturing: transitions can add visual interest to your video and allow viewers to stay oriented for new content

  • Improved storytelling: transitions can help signal a shift in topic or environment in a video

  • Improved visual hierarchy: transitions can reduce the abruptness of new content appearing on the screen

  • Cultivating brand identity: unique, brand-specific transitions that are consistently used can cultivate a sense of identity for your company

Users may feel that your video is more intuitive and be more enticed to return to it. However, there is a very fine balance between subtle emphasis and obnoxiously overwhelming. Transitions can help your users use visual intuition on the flow of information, but using them in an ineffective manner can turn users off your video permanently. Additionally, disabled users may be sensitive to aspects of transitions that are commonly looked over.

Transitions and disability

When designing your transitions, it is important to consider visual appeal and organizational flow. It is also important to consider how these transitions affect disabled users who may not be able to see them or would have an adverse reaction to them.

Some disabilities to consider include:

  • Vestibular disorders (vertigo)

  • Epilepsy

  • Visual impairments

  • Cognitive/Attention disorders

Vestibular and epileptic disorders can be very harmful and easy to trigger. It is imperative that you keep these users in mind when designing quick and flashy transitions, and it is often better to stay away from them. Users who suffer from migraine headaches also could be triggered by unnecessary motion, and the effects could be severe. Users with cognitive and attention disorders like ADHD or autism can be overstimulated and confused. Ensure your video uses purposeful, simple transitions that emphasize clarity, not confusion. Users with visual impairments may be using captions or transcripts. A transition can interrupt the visibility and timing of closed captions, causing the user to incorrectly interpret or even miss information entirely.

WCAG provides guidelines regarding accessible transitions. When designing transitions, your goal should be to ensure users are not harmed or distracted by motion. Eliminate unnecessary motion, unless the animation is essential to functionality. Overall, it is important to remember that people can get sick from your design choices.
