What are title cards and who do they benefit?

Title cards (also referred to as intertitles) are frames of text inserted directly into a video to provide context and structure. Sometimes title cards work to highlight key information in a video, either through summarizing information or reinforcing main points. They also act as a “directory” for the content of the video by clearly segmenting the video into different parts (kind of like chapters in a book, but displayed clearly on the screen).

  • Title cards appear as pictures or frames throughout the video.

  • A popular use of title cards was in silent films. They provided dialogue and context for which there was no audio.

Title cards are a great way to give your video more structure for viewers, and are a simple way to make your content both memorable and enjoyable for your audience.

How do title cards improve digital accessibility?

Title cards provide context throughout a video, and are a great example of design that helps people with disabilities, and creates an overall better user experience for all viewers. When implementing digital accessibility, we want to ensure that all people are able to understand our content. When making videos, it’s important to provide the most context and organization possible. Title cards are a great way to do this.

Title cards can aid deaf users or those hard of hearing with important context throughout the video. By clearly labeling different sections, or taking a moment to summarize key points, title cards allow a “pause” in the script. This can be useful for those with hearing impairments reading dialogue, rather than listening to a regular rhythm of speech. Title cards are also useful for those watching a video in a loud environment by providing clear and distinct markers throughout the video in case they missed something, or need a refresh.

Title cards can also help those with cognitive disabilities by reinforcing main points, therefore making for better retention of information. Studies show audio only information has a retention rate of 10% after 72 hours, but combining it with visual content increases the retention rate to 65% in the same period of time. Title cards can help those with cognitive disabilities by providing mental “breadcrumbs” of the video. These “breadcrumbs” allow them to get a recap of what they’ve already seen or learned, and what’s coming up.

Best practices for creating title cards

Make it readable

  • Ensure your title cards have enough contrast to be legible. Best practice is white or light text on a black, or dark-colored, solid background.The Web Content Accessibility Guidelines (WGAC) requires that all text set to a size of eighteen-points or larger must have a contrast ratio minimum of 3:1 (which means the lighter of the two colors is exactly three times as bright as the darker one). If you’re unsure about your contrast, you can use the WebAIM Contrast Checker.

  • Make sure your font is easy to read. Accessible fonts are typically medium-weight and sans-serif, such as Arial or Helvetica. These fonts are easier for people with visual impairments or dyslexia to read because they reduce visual noise. There are also fonts specifically designed for low vision and readability: Lexend and Atkinson Hyberlegible. Your font should also be large enough to read. For a title card, aim for a minimum of eighteen-point font (WGAC).

Make it useful

The whole point of a title card is to allow viewers to easily jump around a video, and gain a quick understanding about the video’s content.

  • Keep it concise: People should be able to read it quickly.

  • Keep it descriptive: Your title card should still give the viewer a pretty good understanding on what that section is about, or the point you are trying to reiterate. A good way to test this is by asking yourself, "If someone had not watched my video yet, but saw all my title cards, would they have a good understanding of what takes place in the video?"
